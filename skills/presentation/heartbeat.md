# Presentation Skill — Heartbeat

*Preparation is the only thing that makes delivery look effortless.*

---

## When You Get the Request

Run immediately when a presentation is assigned:

"I just found out I need to present [topic] to [audience] on [date]. That gives me [X days]. Help me build a preparation timeline."

The first 24 hours matter most. Decisions made early — about narrative, about scope, about what the presentation is actually trying to accomplish — are much harder to change after slides exist.

---

## Two Weeks Out — Narrative and Structure

"I am building a presentation on [topic] for [audience]. My goal is [outcome]. Help me develop the narrative before I open any software."

At this stage, answer only these questions:
- What does the audience believe before this presentation
- What do you need them to believe or do when it ends
- What are the three to five ideas that connect those two points
- What is the single most important thing they must leave with

Do not open presentation software yet. The structure exists in words before it exists in slides.

---

## One Week Out — Deck and Content

"Here is my presentation outline: [outline]. Help me turn this into a slide structure with one clear idea per slide."

Build in this order:
1. Opening — the hook that earns attention in the first 30 seconds
2. The problem or tension that makes this presentation necessary
3. The body — your three to five key points with supporting evidence
4. The close — the specific action or belief you are asking for
5. The Q&A preparation — questions you will face and your answers

---

## Three Days Out — Speaker Notes and Practice

"Here are my slides: [content]. Write speaker notes that sound like me talking through each one, not reading from them."

Practice checklist:
- Run through the full presentation out loud at least twice
- Time yourself — most people run long, not short
- Practice the opening until it feels natural without notes
- Practice the close until the call to action lands cleanly
- Identify the two or three slides where the content is most complex and practice those specifically

---

## Day Before — Final Preparation

"My presentation is tomorrow. Here is a summary of what I am covering: [summary]. What am I most likely to forget and what should I review tonight?"

Final checklist:
- Slides reviewed on the device you will present from
- Backup copy accessible from a second location
- Room or platform logistics confirmed
- Opening memorized well enough to deliver without looking at notes
- The three questions most likely to come and your answers rehearsed
- What you will do if the technology fails

---

## After the Presentation

"My presentation is done. Here is how it went: [summary of what happened]. What should I capture while it is fresh and what would I do differently?"

Capture immediately:
- Questions that came up you were not prepared for
- Slides that confused the audience
- The moment where energy in the room changed
- What landed better than expected
- The specific follow-up actions that were requested

---

## Follow-Up Within 24 Hours

"I presented [topic] yesterday. The key points were [summary] and the next step I am asking for is [action]. Draft a follow-up email that reinforces the message and makes the next step easy."

A presentation without a follow-up is a conversation that ended at the door. The follow-up is where decisions get made.

---

## Recurring Presentations

For presentations you give regularly — quarterly business reviews, weekly team updates, monthly client reports:

"I give [presentation type] every [frequency]. Help me build a reusable structure so I am not starting from scratch each time."

A template built once saves hours every cycle. The structure stays consistent. Only the content changes.

---

*Confidence in front of a room is not a personality trait. It is the feeling that comes from knowing your material better than anyone in the room needs you to.*
